Fall Trailer Maintenance

Filed under: Practical Towing Info, Trailer Maintenance
Tags: coupler, wheels, tires, wiring, lighting, suspension, trailer, maintenance


In This Article, You Will Learn:
• Trailer Maintenance
 Glossary Terms Used in This Article:
• Coupler


Written by Lee Adelman


For those of us north of the Mason-Dixon Line, summer is quickly drawing to a close, and sadly so are some of our favorite outdoor activities. Septemberis a good time to consider getting our boats, RVs, and other summer fun towables ready for storage. This is also the ideal time to do routine maintenance on your trailers. That way, when spring comes, you can be ready to roll once again without being slowed down by untimely repair work to your trailer.

Here is a basic checklist to ensure that you are road-ready for the spring:

 

1) Coupler - Put some grease on the inside of your coupler and make sure that all moving parts are lubricated. If your trailer is going to be outdoors during the winter, consider placing a plastic bag or something over the coupler and trailer end of the wiring harness. Secure the bag to the frame of the trailer with a zip tie. This will keep moisture and the elements off of your coupler and will help to keep rust away. Also check the attachment hardware for excessive rust or signs of cracking - if you see these, take the time to replace the hardware now.

 

2) Trailer Jack - Grease the moving parts and wrap them with a plastic bag. Check the hardware for rust and cracking and replace suspicious hardware.

 

3) Wheels - This might be a good time to pop off those wheel hubs and re-pack the bearings. If your bearings have nicks or brown spots, (caused by overheating), replace them. If your trailer has brakes, this would also be a good time to make sure that you have plenty of brake-pad left. If not, take the time to change the brakes now.

 

4) Tires - Check for cracking and excessive wear. You do not want to start out next season with a blow-out. I suggest doing what you can to safely keep your trailer's tires off of the ground when the trailer will not be used for long periods of time. Jack stands are a good option for this. Just make sure you have enough and block the jack stands from moving. Keeping the weight off is better for the tires and you'll lose less air pressure over the winter. Even so, you will need to remember to check the air the next time you plan to tow.

 

5) Trailer Wiring/Lighting - Carefully inspect your trailer wiring and lighting. Make sure all connections are solid. Make sure that there are no breaks in the wiring and that there is no exposed copper. If there is, you should take the time to replace those wires now and to make sure that ALL of the functions are working properly. It is a requirement by law that all trailer lights must be functioning.

 

6) Suspension - Make sure that all of the suspension hardware has not rusted out and that your springs and shackles are properly secured to the trailer. If you have any questionable components that are rusting excessively or are beginning to crack, replace them now.

 

If any or all of your components are rusty and you want to remove them, there are many lubricants available at just about any store that you should apply before trying to break the nuts free.

 

Trailer maintenance is usually easy - IF you don't procrastinate on the routine stuff. Take the time NOW to check and fix things. This will go a long way to making sure your activity time is spent playing, and not performing potentially dangerous and costly road-side trailer repair.
