Equestrianism - A Local History

Jul 6, 2011 9:20 am
Posted by Travis Mai

It’s been said that great things can be found in the strangest of places. For the past 15 years I have lived within walking distance of a local horse showmanship club known as the Eau Claire Bit and Spur Club. Every day I drive past it to and from work, giving little thought as to what showmanship, equestrianism, or the history of Bit and Spur really was. I took it upon myself to get the facts in my never-ending quest to become the ultimate authority in towing lifestyle knowledge.

 

A couple of Saturdays ago on my way home I saw what looked liked cowboys circling the arena. This was a little odd to me seeing that normally I just see girls in funny hats and knee high boots (more on this later). I pulled in to take some pictures and listen to what was going on. What I saw amazed me. There were 10-12 riders circling the arena with a man in the center that looked as though he came straight from the late 1800’s…and about as old! He was teaching them something but I didn’t want to get close enough to interrupt. My mind was racing with questions. What was he teaching? Who was he? What’s the difference between his style of riding compared to others I’ve witnessed?

 

In order to continue my quest for towing lifestyle knowledge, I decided that the following week I would get in touch with the Eau Claire Bit and Spur Club. I wrote an email to the club and within an hour I got a response from the club president, Ellen Ness, stating that she’d love to answer any questions I might have about horse showmanship and the Bit & Spur! We then made plans to meet at the following Saturday’s show.

 

My initial mental picture of a bunch of over-privileged teenage girls was quickly swept away the moment I met Ellen. We talked and walked around the grounds as she talked to different girls preparing for their turn to show. Right away she let me in on what was going on the previous weekend. She stated that they offered a clinic on a new training technique that has been sweeping the United States and Europe, called Western Dressage. The instructor of the clinic (man from the 1800’s) was none other than Jack Brainerd. Well into his 90’s, Jack has been training horses for more than 70 years! He is also one of the key figures in organizing and promoting the Western Dressage Association of America. For more on Western Dressage visit http://www.westerndressageassociation.org/

 

After walking the grounds for a bit, Ellen took me in to the lodge for a brief tour through the club’s history.  Walking through this rustic lodge invoked images of Thoreau’s Walden Pond and Laura Ingalls Wilder’s Little House on the Prairie. The Bit and Spur originally started as Polo Club in the 1920’s and since has hosted the families of many of the local social elite such as Charlie Mayo’s family…you know the Medical clinic in Minnesota that even the Pope goes to? In the 1940’s the log cabin style lodge was built on the grounds to be used as a dance hall and dining area for local events. Now they mainly use the lodge as a food venue during the weekend long shows.

 

Once our tour of the lodge was over, Ellen went into the technicalities of showing at the Bit & Spur. My ambition to solve the riddle of the funny hats and knee high boots finally came to a head! She was quick to point out that the attire actually coexists with the riding style. Known as Hunt Seat, it is one of the two classic disciplines (other being dressage) of traditional English riding. Hunt Seat  (based on Fox Hunting) can also be used as a term to describe any forward seat riding. When I asked Ellen how the competitors are judged and scored, she replied that even though there is a 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place, it is really not about human vs. human competition. The real completion comes from within. It is the rider’s ability to cooperate with the horse while performing specific maneuvers in the saddle.

 

Like many organizations, the Bit & Spur has felt the stress of the world wide economic down turn. When asked about future aspirations, Ellen stated that it is really important to her that she does her best keep the youth interested and involved in order to keep the club alive. Along with recruitment and reinforced enthusiasm the Bit & Spur plans to expand its offerings when it comes to riding disciplines. In order to attract a broader spectrum of Equine enthusiasts, they’re planning on utilizing all 40 acres of their compound by offering events featuring trail riding, saddle seat, western dressage, and equine agility.

 

With my brain a little full and still digesting this interesting and rich information, I was able to remember the original reason that I came here in the first place…TOWING!  It was great to hear that Ellen and her husband recently purchased CURT Gooseneck Hitch. When I asked her for some equine specific towing tips and suggestions she stated that it would be easier for her just to tell a story. She spoke of an incident in which one winter’s day she and her husband where planning on hauling their horses to their trainer’s facility for a lesson. They hooked the trailer to their receiver hitch and backed into position. Strangely enough the horses were dead set on staying out of the trailer. After a while of tugging and pulling the horses towards the trailer they were finally able to get them in and close the gate. With the horses all loaded up, they continued on to their trainer’s place. Before getting on the highway her husband came to a complete stop and looked at her and said “You know…you’re going to think this is kind of odd, but I’m going out there to check that hitch.” At first she was skeptical because of his past history of being somewhat of a worry wart, but sure enough he came back to the truck and reluctantly stated “I don’t know how it happened, but someone took the receiver pin and the ball mount has been slowly working its way out of the receiver.” Both thankful and frustrated the couple sat in disbelief that they could have missed something so simple. Moments later another couple stopped to see if they needed any help. Sure enough they had a spare pin and clip and saved the day!-Gotta love Wisconsin hospitality!

 

While I’m sure I’ll never be able to consider myself an equine expert, I really learned a lot from Ellen about horse showing and about a local historical treasure that I overlooked. After this eye-opening experience I will never let an opportunity like this pass me by again.
